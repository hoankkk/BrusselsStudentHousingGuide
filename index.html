Chapter One

Domiciliation &
Residency Registration

For non-EEA students, Belgian residence rules are strictly separate from housing registration. Understanding this distinction is essential before you arrive.

The Domicile vs. Student Housing Rule

In the Brussels-Capital Region, standard student accommodations are designated for study purposes only and are legally not recognised as a "principal residence." This has important consequences for non-EEA students.

No Official Domicile in Student Housing

Official domicile registration (domiciliation) is generally not permitted in dedicated student housing. These contracts specifically exclude the "principal residence" provisions of the Brussels Housing Code.

Instead, international students may register their address as a "place of establishment" (lieu d'établissement) if required by local authorities — provided their official permanent domicile remains in their home country.

  • Mandatory proof of enrollment: To maintain legal status under a student lease, you must provide a valid enrollment certificate from a recognised educational institution upon signing and at every renewal.
  • Home country domicile: Your official permanent domicile must remain registered in your home country for the duration of your studies.
  • Right to register an address: Any clause in a rental contract that prohibits a tenant from registering their residential address is legally void under the Brussels Housing Code.
Legal Framework for Stay

Stays of More Than 90 Days

Any non-EEA foreigner authorised to stay in Belgium for more than three months must be inscribed in the Foreigner's Register (registre des étrangers) by the municipal administration.

  • Stay Authorisation: You must apply for authorisation via a Belgian diplomatic or consular post in your home country before travelling.
  • Financial Means: You must demonstrate sufficient financial resources for subsistence throughout your stay.
  • Health Insurance: Valid comprehensive health insurance coverage is required.
  • Clean Criminal Record: A certificate of good conduct from your home country may be requested.
Step-by-Step Process

Registration Process — Visa D & A-Card

If you hold a Visa D (long-stay visa with a restrictive provision for studies), you must register with your commune to obtain your A-Card (Carte A).

  • Book an Appointment with Municipal Administration
    Depending on your commune, an appointment may or may not be required. Check your specific municipality's website. For Brussels 1000, contact the student registration office directly.
  • Attend Your Appointment with Required Documents
    Bring all of the following without exception — missing any document will result in a rescheduled appointment.
  • Police Residence Verification
    After your appointment, a local police officer will visit your address to verify that you actually reside there. You must be present. This is a standard step and not a cause for concern.
  • Second Appointment & A-Card Issuance
    Once the police confirm your residence, a second appointment is automatically scheduled by the Municipal Administration to finalise your registration and order your Residence Permit (A-Card).
Documents Required at Appointment
  • National Passport with a valid Visa D
  • School Registration Certificate — must be the required standard form from your institution
  • Passport Photo — 1 recent photo
  • Registration Fee — approximately €35

Chapter Two

Finding Student
Housing in Brussels

A student room in Belgium is commonly called a kot. Brussels offers several routes — from university-managed residences to the private market — each with its own trade-offs.

University Residences

Check with your institution's housing office first and as early as possible. Universities such as ULB (Université Libre de Bruxelles) and VUB (Vrije Universiteit Brussel) operate their own on-campus or near-campus residences — generally the most affordable option available.

Apply Immediately Upon Admission

Demand for university residences is very high. Do not wait until your visa is confirmed — submit your housing application as soon as you receive your offer of admission.

Typical price range: €350 – €550 / month, often with utilities included.

Charges: TTC vs HC

When reading ads, always check whether the price is TTC (Toutes Taxes Comprises — all-inclusive) or HC (Hors Charges — excluding utilities). Utilities typically add €100–€150/month if not included.

Neighbourhood Overview

Areas to Know

Brussels is a patchwork city — a "good" neighbourhood can have a rough street three blocks away. "Unsafe" is always relative, but some areas are consistently flagged by students.

Higher-Cost, Well-Connected Areas

Ixelles (Flagey/Châtelain), Etterbeek (near the EU Quarter), and Woluwe-Saint-Lambert are popular and well-served but carry a neighbourhood premium on rent.

Areas Requiring Extra Caution for Housing

Gare du Midi surroundings (towards Anderlecht/Kuregem): higher rates of petty crime, especially at night.

Gare du Nord surroundings: borders the Red Light District (Rue d'Aerschot); can feel desolate at night.

Lower/Central Molenbeek (near Ribaucourt or Beekkant metro): higher instances of street harassment. Note: Upper Molenbeek near Scheutbos is green and residential.

Anderlecht (Kuregem/Clemenceau): cheaper rent, but street safety concerns at night. Anderlecht is large — ask fellow students for their specific experience.

Saint-Josse-ten-Noode: very central and affordable, but some parts near Place Madou or Place Rogier can be chaotic and noisy.

Fraud Prevention

Recognising & Avoiding Rental Fraud

Fake listings are a persistent problem. Fraudulent "landlords" — often claiming to be abroad — convince students to transfer large sums to foreign bank accounts under the guise of securing accommodation. Once payment is made, they disappear.

  • !
    Too good to be true: If the rent is significantly lower than comparable listings, it almost certainly is a scam.
  • !
    No direct contact possible: You should always be able to meet the landlord or schedule a video call. Persistent excuses to avoid this are a major red flag.
  • !
    Payment before contract: Never transfer money before signing a contract. Requests for payment via Western Union, MoneyGram, or cryptocurrency are almost always fraudulent.
  • !
    Unsolicited ID requests: You may need to present ID when signing a contract, but never send a copy without being certain the landlord and property are genuine. Fraudsters misuse ID for identity theft.
  • !
    Stolen photos: Run a Google Reverse Image Search on all property photos to check if they appear elsewhere online or belong to a different listing.
  • !
    No written contract: A rental agreement is a legal requirement in Belgium. Never occupy any accommodation without a signed contract.
If You Are a Victim of Fraud

File a formal police report immediately. If you are uncertain about a landlord, contact KU Leuven Stuvo before transferring any money or signing any document. You can also report suspected fraud (even if you haven't fallen victim yet) to the Belgian authorities to help prevent further scams.

III
Chapter Three

The Student
Rental Contract

A "Student Housing Lease" (Bail de logement étudiant) is a specific legal category under the Brussels Housing Code. Both landlords and tenants have binding obligations from the moment of signing.

Pre-Contractual Disclosure

Before a lease is signed, the landlord is legally required to provide the following to any prospective tenant. These details must also appear in any public advertisement for the property.

  • Property details: Exact address, floor, habitable surface area, and the official indicative reference rent.
  • Rent amount: Stated separately — it cannot include utility charges.
  • Itemised charges: An estimated list of common and private charges (water, gas, electricity, etc.).
  • Utility infrastructure: Whether individual meters exist, and if so, their EAN identification codes.
  • Energy Performance Certificate (PEB/EPC): The certified energy rating of the property.
  • Building management: How the building is managed (syndic, owner-managed, etc.).
  • Legal status: Clear disclosure if the property has been banned from rental due to safety or health violations.
Mandatory Contract Content

What the Contract Must Contain

To qualify as a student lease, the tenant must provide proof of enrollment in a secondary or higher education institution. The written contract must include:

  • Identity of all parties: Full legal names and contact details of both landlord and tenant.
  • Start date: The official first day of the tenancy.
  • Property description: Exact address, floor, habitable surface area, and reference rent.
  • Rent amount: Stated separately from all charges — this separation is a fundamental legal principle.
  • Reference rent: From the official indicative grid, where available.
  • Charges: Itemised list and estimate of common and private charges, specifying whether each is a flat rate (forfait) or based on actual costs (frais réels).
  • Utility meters: Whether individual meters exist, including EAN codes.
  • Duration: Generally 12 months or less for student leases, with clear start and end dates. If a tenant remains after the term without landlord opposition, the contract is typically extended by one year.
Mandatory Government Annex

A government-drafted legal annex explaining safety standards, rent indexation rules, charge regulations, repair obligations, and termination procedures must be physically attached to every lease. A contract without this annex is non-compliant.

Financial Obligations

Security Deposit

A landlord may request a rental guarantee to cover potential unpaid obligations at the end of the lease.

  • Maximum amount: As of 1 November 2024, the deposit cannot exceed two months' rent.
  • Placement: Must be held in a frozen, individualised bank account in the tenant's name. The landlord holds a lien on the account but cannot access funds without consent or a court order.
  • Return: The full deposit plus any accrued interest is returned to the tenant upon satisfactory completion of all obligations — verified by joint written agreement or a court ruling.
  • Gradual build-up option: A bank guarantee allowing the tenant to build the deposit through fixed monthly payments over a maximum of three years is also accepted.
Financial Assistance

The Fonds du Logement (Housing Fund) provides financial support and professional guidance to help tenants establish a rental deposit if needed.

Taxes & Charges — What You May Owe

The key principle: a tax is only payable by the tenant if it is explicitly named in the signed contract. If it is not listed, the landlord cannot demand payment.

  • Property Tax (précompte immobilier): This is the landlord's responsibility alone and cannot be passed to the tenant under a student lease — any contract clause attempting to do so is void.
  • Secondary Residence Tax (Taxe sur les résidences non-principales): Applied when you live in a municipality without being officially registered. Can be as high as €2,700/year. Many communes (including Schaerbeek) offer exemptions for full-time students.
  • Furnished Dwelling Tax (Taxe sur les logements garnis): In the City of Brussels, landlords are taxed €500/year (indexed) for furnished units. This can be passed to the tenant only if the contract specifies it explicitly. Student exemptions do not apply automatically for this tax.
  • Waste Collection Tax (Taxe sur les immondices): A regional charge for waste collection, commonly included in the "charges" section of student contracts.
Practical Obligations

Inventory, Registration & Insurance

  • Joint Inventory (État des Lieux): A detailed written inspection must be completed jointly by both parties — at move-in and move-out. Cost is typically shared equally. This document is the legal basis for any damage claims at the end of the lease.
  • Lease Registration: The landlord must register the contract with the Regional Lease Registration Service within two months of signing. Registration is free. If the landlord fails to register within this period, the tenant may terminate the lease without notice or penalty, and the landlord loses the right to index the rent.
  • Fire & Water Damage Insurance (Mandatory): Tenants are legally responsible for fire or water damage unless they can prove they were not at fault. You must obtain an insurance policy before entry and provide annual proof of payment. Some platforms (e.g., MyKot) include this insurance directly in the contract.
  • Waiver of Recourse (Abandon de Recours): If a tenant fails to obtain insurance, the landlord may add a waiver of recourse clause to their own building insurance and charge the cost to the tenant.
  • Contents Insurance (Recommended): Covers your personal belongings (laptop, phone, furniture). Not always legally required, but frequently stipulated in contracts.
Penalties for Non-Compliance

Legal Consequences

Landlord Liability
Failure to Register Lease
Loses the right to index the rent. Tenant may terminate without notice or penalty.
Landlord Liability
Renting Unsafe / Banned Housing
Fines ranging from €2,000 to €25,000 under the Housing Code.

Illegal Eviction
Evicting without a court order makes the landlord liable for an indemnity equal to 18 months' rent.
Tenant Liability
Serious Breach of Contract
Two months of rent arrears or failure to pay the deposit can lead to judicial annulment of the contract at the tenant's expense.
